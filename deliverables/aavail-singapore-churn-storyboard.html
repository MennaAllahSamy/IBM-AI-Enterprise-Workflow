Act I — The data & the opportunity

What we're looking at

The analysis covers 1,000 AAVAIL customers across two markets. Each record carries an ID and name, the customer's age, home market (United States or Singapore), whether they are an active subscriber, their plan tier (Basic, Premium, Unlimited), and their monthly streaming volume.

Before any conclusions, the two columns with gaps were repaired with mechanism-specific imputation: plan tier (missing at random) was filled by sampling each market's own observed plan mix, and streaming volume (missing in a pattern tied to market and plan) was filled with a market-by-plan median. The fills shifted the underlying distributions by less than a third of a percentage point, so the story below rests on the real shape of the data.

The business opportunity

Singapore is 30% of the customer base but the weakest link in retention. Closing even part of the gap between Singapore's 60% churn and the US's 15% would convert a large block of already-acquired customers into recurring revenue — growth that costs nothing in new acquisition. The question that pays for itself: why are Singapore customers leaving?

Act II — The investigation

Following the leak, one suspect at a time

We started with the obvious question — is this really a Singapore problem, or noise? — then lined up the usual suspects for churn and tested each against the data.

First: is the market difference real?

Yes, and it's large. A chi-square test links market to churn at p < 10⁻⁴⁵, with a Cramér's V of 0.45 — a strong association, not a rounding artifact.

With the market difference confirmed, we drilled into Singapore alone and asked what separates the customers who stay from those who leave.

Ruled out

Suspect 1 — Age

Do older or younger Singapore customers leave more? No meaningful difference: leavers and stayers are the same age.

Mann–Whitney p = 0.35 · Cohen's d = −0.07 (negligible)
Ruled out

Suspect 2 — Engagement (streaming volume)

The intuitive culprit — people who don't watch, leave. But in Singapore, leavers stream almost as much as stayers. Low usage is not what's driving churn.

Mann–Whitney p = 0.18 · Cohen's d = 0.14 (negligible)
Confirmed

Suspect 3 — Plan tier

Here the data breaks open. In Singapore, churn climbs with price: Basic customers leave 40% of the time, Premium 69%, Unlimited 76%. In the US, the same tiers churn at a flat 13–18% — plan tier barely matters there.

Chi-square plan × churn (SG) p < 0.001
Act III — Findings in context

A price–value gap, not a product problem

The pattern points to one explanation: in Singapore, the higher-priced plans aren't delivering enough perceived value to justify their cost. Customers on Premium and Unlimited — the plans AAVAIL most wants to grow — are exactly the ones walking away. Since engagement is healthy, this reads as a pricing and packaging mismatch specific to the Singapore market, not a content or usability failure.

The model agrees

A logistic regression on all 1,000 customers isolates each factor's independent pull on churn. Being in Singapore multiplies churn odds by 9.7×; sitting on a Premium or Unlimited plan roughly doubles them — while age and streaming volume have no significant effect once market and plan are accounted for.

A random-forest churn model tells the same story from a different angle — it separates leavers from stayers at ROC-AUC 0.74, and ranks market and Unlimited-plan membership as its top two signals, echoing the regression.

Who exactly is leaving

Unsupervised segmentation groups the customers into four natural clusters. One stands apart: a near-entirely-Singapore segment (97%) carrying a 58% churn rate — more than triple the calm, US-heavy segments beside it. This is the block to act on.

Act IV — Discussion & next steps

What to do about it

The evidence converges on Singapore's premium pricing. The recommendations below move from fastest test to deepest fix.

1

Re-price and re-package the premium tiers in Singapore

Run an A/B pricing test on Premium and Unlimited in Singapore — lower entry price, localized bundles, or a value add-on — and measure retention against the current tiers. This targets the exact plans doing the damage.

2

Ask the leavers directly

Survey or interview churned Singapore Premium/Unlimited customers. The data shows where value breaks down; qualitative signal will explain why — price sensitivity, missing local content, or payment friction.

3

Instrument real churn, not a snapshot

This analysis uses subscription status as a churn proxy at a single point in time. Add timestamps — signup, cancellation, plan changes — so churn can be measured as an event and the pricing tests above can be evaluated on true retention curves.

Read this with care

The churn measure is a snapshot proxy. Without a time dimension, "churn" here means "currently not an active subscriber," which blends never-converted and lapsed customers. The market and plan-tier signals are strong and stable, but the precise churn magnitudes should be confirmed once longitudinal data lands (step 3). A handful of records also list a plan for a non-subscriber; these were kept as-is and flagged rather than silently reclassified.
